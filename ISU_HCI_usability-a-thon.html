Usability-a-thon Winning Project

Iowa State: HCI 2019 Competition

My Deliverables User Interviews, Usability Testing Reports, Clickable Prototype (Photoshop and Python)
Team Me (Technical Communication, Bachelor's), Jiztom (Machine Learning, PhD ), Karthick (Industrial Engineering Master's), Guilherme (Business, Bachelor's)
Duration 8 hours (April 20, 2019)

Competition Background

Every Spring, the Iowa State Human Computer Interaction (HCI) graduate program puts on a Usability-a-thon, a hack-a-thon style event where graduate and undergraduate students are teamed up to define and solve a problem. Non-competing, student volunteers also come to the event to be participants for user testing.

I was teamed up with 3 random strangers, Jiztom, Karthick, and Guilhermen. We were tasked to solve a problem that helped create trust between the user and a company.

In 8 hours, we created a competition winning case studying on why Iowa State should change the information architecture of AccessPlus so students can more easily change their privacy settings.






Our Problem Statement

The Iowa State University public directory houses student emails and phone numbers are available to the public by default. Many students first learn about this friends, classmates, or the first time someone contacts them before they have given out their contact information. This personal information can be difficult to remove because of the location of the settings to turn them off.

Audience

Iowa State Students

Audience Goal

More easily change their contact information privacy settings

Our Challenges

Time - we only had 8 hours

Lack of knowledge about business constraints

Our Solution

Change the information architecture on AccessPlus so the privacy settings are more easily found

Learning About The Problem

My Story

My junior year of college (about a month before the competion), I was emailed by a man, a non-student, who lived in Ames, IA. I had met him the day prior when he would not leave me alone at a club promotion event. He only knew my first name and my major. I even lied about what year I was, yet he was still able to find my Iowa State email address on the Iowa State Student Directory.

Luckily for me, the man was harmless and meant well, but I was terrifed and angry that I didn't know this could happen. I told my friends (both men and women) and many of them had similar stories. They had either been contacted by a non-student or a classmate who could not take a hint without giving away their contact information.

The Problem

  1. Lack of Information: I didn't know my email and phone number were public by default.
  2. Bad Directions: It took me a very long time to figure out how to change my privacy settings.

User Opinions On Their Data Privacy

My team and I wanted to formally interview some Iowa State students to confirm our suspicions about the lack of and learn more about student preferences. As a team, we formed several hypotheses and created list of questions to help gather qualitative data about the following:



What types of contact information students were fine with being available to the public and to other students.



How students would want to change their privacy settings.



If students knew their contact information was available to the pubic and if so, how they found out



If the university shoulddo more to inform students about the Iowa State public directory


Hypothesis:

  1. Most students don't remember being told by the university that their email and phone number is available to the public.
  2. Most students do not want their email or phone number to be available to the public
  3. Most students will be fine with their emails being accessible to other students, but not their phone number.

Test Users

In order to particapate in our test, users had to be Iowa State students who were familiar with AccessPlus.

5 undergrad students, 1 transfer student, 3 graduate students

Results

To make the best use of our VERY limited time, we broke up the work and each interview 2 students. Here is what we found:

  • Most students had not been told by the university that their phone number and email . One student remembered an email from the university, the other seven learned about it from friends or classmates.
  • More than half of students are fine with their emails to available to Iowa State students, but not their phone number.
  • More than half of students do not want their phone number or email available to the general public.
  • 100% of students wanted to be able to control their privacy settings themselves.

Brainstorming Solutions

We knew that our solution had to allow students to control their own settings and it had to be visible enough that any student could see it. To come up with a solution, we wrote anything we could think of down on a sticky note with the philosophy that there was no bad ideas. Then, as a group, we decided our top 3 solutions and listed out the pros and cons of each.

We ended up deciding to test solution 1, changing the navigation. You can see the origional flow under the "Testing the Current Website" section and the flow we came up with under the "Building the Prototype" section.


Idea 1

Update AccessPlus Navigation to increase privacy setting visability


Pros
1. Solves the larger problem
2. All students (even freshman) know how to use AccessPlus

Cons
1. Possible unknown business constraints
2. Already lots of side navigation elements, possible findability problem


Idea 2

Update Instructions on Student Directory


Pros
1. Very easy to implement, low tech

Cons
1. Students may not learn about the directory for a few months or more into college
2. Students may not read the directions


Idea 3

Remove the filtering section on the Student Directory


Pros
1. Makes it harder for students to be found, unless you know them

Cons
1. Students may have the same name/major/classification

Testing the Current Website

Along with the student volunteers, there were also UX professions to help with mentoring and user testing. We asked the employees to be our test users becuase we wanted to test on users who had little knowledge about the website. While they weren't the target audience, they would have the same amount of knowledge about AccessPlus as incoming freshman.

We broke up into teams of two and tested two years each. For my team, I took notes while Guilherme asked the questions and Karthick took notes while Jiztom asked the questions. We asked our users to think out loud while they were doing the task.

Building the Prototype

During task 1 of testing, no one could find the settings page. When given directions from the ISU directory telling them to go to the "Address Change" tab, 3 out of 4 users were able to find how to change their settings. The user who did not sucessfully complete the second task struggled to see the "ISU Directory privacy options" link.

To solve both of these problems, we removed the extra step of going to the "Address Change" tab by giving "Privacy Settings" it's own place in the side navigation.

To actually build a prototype to test, I photoshopped some screenshots of AccessPlus. I sent them to Jiztom, who used python to make them interactive.

What I learned?

Some problems might be a symptom of something larger. The problem my team choose to solve wasn't a simple one. There were multiple good solutions to the problem, but we needed to think about what was cause the problem as well. In this case, the problem wasn't that someone emailed me. It was that I didn't know it was possible in the first place.

Not all user experience problems need a highly technical solution. Something as simple as making directions simpler and more visual can be an easy to implement solution.

The user's experience can start before they even touch their phone or computer. Technology is never designed in isolation. It starts with a problem someone is facing in the real world.

Storytelling is key. While I think my team's idea and our UX process was really well thoughtout, so were the other team's ideas. We ended up getting first, instead of second or third, because we were able to use our qualitative and quantitative to tell a story.
